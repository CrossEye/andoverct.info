Saturday evening (1) — the thesis
---------------------------------


### Saturday evening — The thesis ###

Image: `01_thesis.png`, `01_chart_growth.png`.

Lead with the stat card; the growth chart is the optional second image.

```
Another mailer landed in Andover mailboxes this week, urging a NO vote on Tuesday's school budget.

The last one leaned on arithmetic that didn't hold up. This one leans on a single word: "unsustainable." So we checked it against the town's own record.

Connecticut publishes per-pupil spending for every district, every year. Over the last five years, Andover raised its by 16.9%. The typical comparable district raised its by 24.8%, and the cost of living rose 23.3%. Andover grew slower than 35 of its 44 peer districts.

A budget that grows slower than the cost of living, and slower than almost all of its peers, is not unsustainable. That is restraint.

The facts are at andoverct.info/the-facts.
```


Sunday (3) — the numbers
------------------------


### Sunday, morning — The 6% is not your tax bill ###

Image: `02_tax.png`.

```
The mailer wants you to react to "6%." Here is what that number actually is.

6% is the Andover Elementary School budget alone, one piece of the ballot. Put the whole town budget together and it rises 3.4%. Your estimated tax rate goes up three-tenths of one percent (0.30%).

The 6% is real. It is just not the number on your tax bill. The figure being used to alarm you is not the figure you pay.
```


### Sunday, midday — Drawing on the reserve is normal ###

Image: `03_reserve.png`.

```
The mailer treats the school's plan to use its reserve as a warning sign. It has the account backwards.

Connecticut lets school districts keep a nonlapsing account (Conn. Gen. Stat. 10-248a) for exactly this purpose. Andover's balance, $346,709 at the last audit, is the product of years of finishing under budget and banking a part of the difference. The school is drawing on it now to cover a specific cut the Board of Finance made last year. Afterward, the account still holds about a quarter of a million dollars.

And the town does the same thing. Its own budget plans to draw $100,000 from its fund balance next year, after $190,000 this year and $150,000 the year before. Using a reserve to balance a budget is ordinary practice in Andover, not a red flag.
```


### Sunday, evening — A NO vote cuts nothing ###

Image: `04_novote.png`.

Ends on a question, unannounced. That hanging question is the quiet pivot into
Monday.

```
Suppose the budget fails on Tuesday. What does a NO vote actually accomplish?

Not new scrutiny. The Board of Finance held public meetings on this budget. The Board of Education published its packets. The audited statements are public. The boards heard the objections, weighed them, and reduced the budget twice. That work is done.

A NO vote doesn't reopen any of it. It schedules another referendum, a few weeks later, on essentially the same budget, and asks the rest of us to pay for running it.

So if a NO vote changes nothing, why is anyone pushing it this hard?
```


Monday (3) — the man behind the mailer
--------------------------------------


### Monday, morning — Who is asking you to vote NO ###

Image: `05_whosasking.png`.

```
The mailer urging a NO vote is signed by Jeffrey Maguire.  (Or is it Jeffery?  Even after a few mailers he seems confused about this.) It does not mention that he is Andover's First Selectman.

Under the town charter, the First Selectman sits *ex officio* on every board, including Finance and Education. He was in the room as this budget was built. He could ask questions, request data, and weigh in at every stage. He did. The boards considered his objections and produced this budget anyway.

So this is not the town speaking. It is the town's chief elected official, spending private money to overturn the work of the boards he himself sits on, in a mailer that never mentions the office he holds.
```

Cross-posting to civics-only groups
-----------------------------------

For the civics-only groups, the safe finales are the pure mechanics: polls are
open, here is where and when. "Vote YES" may read as politics depending on the
group's norms, so keep those versions to a vote-reminder framing. On the
private-group sharing question: a private group's setting is what blocks
external sharing, and that applies to the copy as it lives in the group. Posting
to your own public profile first and then sharing that into the group keeps the
profile original publicly shareable, while the in-group copy is just a pointer.
The tradeoff is attribution: a profile post reads as you personally, not the
committee. Verify Facebook's current group-sharing behavior before relying on
the specifics.
