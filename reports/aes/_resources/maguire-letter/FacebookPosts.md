Post 1 — The math (~120 words)

If you got Jeff Maguire's mailer, the "$81,833 per additional student" number is doing a lot of work. Worth checking.

He took the 3-year budget increase ($1.2M) and divided by the 3-year enrollment change (15 students). That's not per-student cost. By the same math, a household that got a $30K raise over three years and had a new baby would say that baby costs $30,000 a year.

Andover's actual per-student AES spending under the proposed budget: about $26,500. In line with Connecticut elementary norms.

Post 4 — State data (~160 words)

Selectman Maguire's mailer calls Andover's AES spending growth "unreasonable and unsustainable." Connecticut's own data says he's wrong.

The state publishes Net Current Expenditures Per Pupil (NCEP) for every district every year, the official measure. For 2024-25:

Andover: $24,643

Comparable elementary-only districts (average): $27,119

Andover spent less per pupil than 16 of 21 comparable districts statewide. Over five years, Andover's per-pupil cost grew 9.8%. The peer-group average grew 18.3%.

AES hasn't been a runaway-spending district. It's been one of the slowest-growing elementary districts in its peer group, holding the per-pupil line while inflation ate into purchasing power.
Selectman Maguire had access to all of this data throughout the budget process. He didn't include any of it in his mailer.

Source: CT SDE Bureau of Fiscal Services. Public, free, available to anyone.

Post 5 — The inflation reframe (~170 words)

One more from the Maguire mailer.

"Unreasonable and unsustainable" is the framing. Here's what five years of Andover budgets actually looked like:

Inflation, Jan 2020 → Jan 2026: +23.1%

Social Security COLA (cumulative): +28.8%

Andover total town budget, FY 2020-21 → FY 2025-26: +2.9%

Two-point-nine percent. Over five years. Total town budget, General Government, AES, and RHAM levy combined.

A retiree on Social Security saw their benefits rise ten times faster than the entire Andover tax burden over the same period.

Even with the proposed 2026-27 budget, six-year cumulative growth is 9.2%, still far under inflation (~32%) and COLA (~33%).

The mailer has the story upside down. Andover has been absorbing below-inflation budgets for years. Selectman Maguire was First Selectman for some of them, and ex officio at many relevant meetings this year.

The case for restraint isn't the case he's making. It's already the case the town has been living.

Post 6 — What a NO vote actually does (~190 words) (save for Tuesday morning)

Last one on the budget.

Selectman Maguire's mailer frames a NO vote as a vote for "accountability, transparency, and fiscal responsibility." It isn't. A NO vote doesn't produce any of those things. It produces another vote, on essentially the same budget, in a few weeks.

Last year's first referendum failed. The BOE came back with a budget $163,694 lower, about 1.2% of the total. That's what a re-vote looks like in practice.

The accountability and scrutiny Mr. Maguire says he wants? They already happened. The Board of Finance held public meetings. The BOE published monthly packets. The audit was filed and is public. Mr. Maguire, as ex officio member of both boards, was in those rooms raising concerns directly with the people doing the work.

The BOF and BOE listened, considered, and produced this budget. A NO vote doesn't reopen that process. It just runs it back, faster, before producing essentially the same result, and asks the rest of us to pay for the second referendum.

If you want real scrutiny, show up to a BOF meeting. The next one is public.

Vote YES on May 26.
